Resources

In recent years, there has been a tremendous demand for atomically flat thin films across a wide range of industries, including electronics, photonics, energy generation and storage, medical, biomedical, and aerospace. Defect-free, conformal, and uniform thin films are essential for ensuring consistent material performance in these applications, and atomically flat films play a vital role in modern nanofabrication.

Atomic layer deposition is one such technique that stands out for several reasons, including precise thickness control, excellent film conformality, low-temperature processing, stoichiometric control, and inherent film quality. The demand for continuous and pinhole-free films in semiconductor devices has further driven developments in ALD. The International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors (ITRS) has also recognized ALD for applications such as high-dielectric-constant gate oxides in MOSFET structures and copper diffusion barriers for interconnects.

The demand for continuous and pinhole-free films in semiconductor devices has driven the development of Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD). ALD enables the precise formation of ultra-thin, conformal passivation or capping layers, ensuring effective protection against environmental degradation and surface contamination. ALD effectively meets the requirements for atomic-level control and conformal deposition through sequential, self-limiting surface reactions.

Most ALD methods rely on binary reaction sequences, where two surface reactions occur to deposit a binary compound film. Since there are a limited number of surface sites, the reactions can deposit only a finite number of surface species. When both surface reactions are self-limiting, they proceed sequentially, enabling the deposition of thin films with atomic-level precision. The primary advantages of ALD include precise thickness control at the Angstrom or monolayer level.

The self-limiting nature of ALD ensures excellent step coverage and conformal deposition on high aspect ratio structures. Initially, some surface areas react before others due to varying precursor gas fluxes. However, the precursors adsorb and subsequently desorb from fully reacted areas, then proceed to react with unreacted surface areas, resulting in highly conformal deposition. This self-limiting aspect also leads to non-statistical deposition eliminating randomness in precursor flux as a significant factor. Consequently, ALD films remain extremely smooth and conformal to the original substrate, as the reactions are driven to completion during each cycle. Since no surface sites are left unreacted during film growth, the resulting films are continuous and pinhole-free, which is crucial for depositing high-quality dielectric films.

ALD is also scalable to very large substrates and suitable for parallel processing of multiple substrates. The gas-phase ALD precursors fill all available space, regardless of substrate geometry, and do not require line-of-sight to the substrate. The process is limited only by the size of the reaction chamber. Dominated by surface reactions, ALD ensures that the two gas-phase reactants do not contact each other in the gas phase, minimizing the formation of particles that could deposit on the surface and produce granular films.

XPS is a surface sensitive analytical technique used to determine the elemental composition and chemical states of the materials. XPS is based on the photoelectric effect. When a material is irradiated with monochromatic X-rays (commonly Al Kα or Mg Kα), core-level electrons are ejected from atoms in the sample. The kinetic energy (KE) of these emitted electrons is measured. The binding energy (BE) of the electrons is then calculated using the equation, Binding Energy = hν - KE – Ф, where hν is photon energy of the X ray, KE is the kinetic energy of emitted electrons and Ф is the work function of spectrometer. Each element has characteristic binding energies, allowing elemental identification and analysis of oxidation states.

X-ray Diffraction (XRD) was employed to investigate the crystallographic structure, phase composition, and degree of crystallinity of the deposited thin films. XRD is a non-destructive analytical technique based on the diffraction of monochromatic X-rays by the periodic atomic planes within a crystalline material. When X-rays are incident on a sample, constructive interference occurs at specific angles that satisfy Bragg’s law which is expressed as, nλ = 2dsinθ, where n is the order of diffraction (usually 1), λ is wavelength, d is the interplanar spacing and θ is angle of incidence. By measuring the angles at which these diffraction peaks appear, the spacing between atomic planes is determined and crystal structure is identified.

Spectroscopic ellipsometry is a non-destructive and highly sensitive optical technique used to determine the thickness and complex refractive index (n and k) of thin films. The method is based on analyzing the change in polarization state of linearly polarized light upon reflection from the sample surface. It measures two parameters—Ψ, the amplitude ratio, and Δ, the phase difference—between the p- and s-polarized components of reflected light. In this study, measurements were performed on J. A Woollam iSE ellipsometer over a spectral range of 300–1000 nm at incident angle of 65 ° to obtain the wavelength-dependent optical response. To extract the optical constants and film thickness, the experimental Ψ and Δ spectra were modeled using a combination of the B-spline approach and the Kramers-Kronig (K-K) consistent model. The B-spline method provides a flexible, model-independent way to fit the dispersion of n and k, while enforcing Kramers-Kronig relations ensures the derived optical constants obey causality and physical consistency. This hybrid modeling approach enables accurate characterization of materials with complex or unknown optical behavior, making spectroscopic ellipsometry a powerful tool for thin film and nanomaterial analysis.
